## What was the study really about?

When you fold a paper strip, the hinges are linked by the paper.  Changing
one hinge can cause other hinges to bend too — this is called
**coupling**.

We wanted to know: **do people mentally simulate this coupling when they
imagine a fold?**  In other words, when you picked which hinges would move,
were your choices shaped by the physical rules of the strip?

## Our main prediction

We predicted that **the more hinges a strip has, the stronger the coupling
effect in mental simulation**.  This is because longer strips have more links
between hinges.

We checked this by comparing the hinges you clicked with the hinges that are
coupled in the physical model.

## Why does this matter?

Knowing how the mind simulates physical objects is important for:

- **Cognitive science**: It tells us how spatial thinking works.
- **Design**: People who design machines need to mentally simulate how parts move.
- **Education**: It may help us teach origami, engineering, and spatial thinking.

## What about the questionnaires?

You filled in two questionnaires:

- **AQ-10**: A short measure of autistic-trait thinking.  People vary in how
  they mentally simulate motion, and autistic traits are one part of this.
- **MRT (Mental Rotation Test)**: A measure of your ability to rotate 3D
  objects in your mind.

We will use these as *covariates* — not to label you, but to account for
personal differences in our analysis.

## Was there any deception?

No.  We told you this was a study about mental folding, and it was.  We only
hid the specific prediction we were testing, so that knowing it would not
change your answers.
